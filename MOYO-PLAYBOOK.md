## 1. What Moyo is

**Moyo is a one-person web studio based in Harare, Zimbabwe, building websites for African law firms, NGOs, civil society organisations, and professional services businesses.**

The word means *heart* in Shona. It names the thing that separates work that merely functions from work that carries the weight of what an organisation actually is.

The practice exists because two failure modes are endemic in the market:

- Western developers don't understand the context — what "credible to London" means to a Harare law firm, or what a donor programme officer looks for in a pan-African NGO site
- Local developers often don't deliver at the quality level these institutions need

Moyo sits in the gap. It is not an agency. It is not a generic freelancer. It is a named practice with a defined niche, a body of work, and a consistent standard — operated by one person who does the work personally.

**The one-person-does-the-work fact is a feature, not a bug.** The person a client speaks to at the start is the person who builds their site. Say this clearly and often.

### What Moyo does

- Institutional web presence for African organisations
- Sites that open doors with international funders, partners, clients, and investors
- Fast, accessible, professionally crafted code — no templates, no page builders

### What Moyo does not do

- Logo design or full brand identity
- Social media management
- Mobile app development
- General IT support
- Projects for clients who just want "something cheap online"

## 2. Philosophy

### The core belief

**Every serious African institution deserves a web presence that matches the seriousness of its work.**

Most don't have one. That gap costs them — in donor funding not received, in commercial partnerships not formed, in professional credibility not established with the counterparties that matter. Moyo closes that gap.

### What "heart" means in practice

The name is not decoration. It is a commitment:

- To understand a client's actual mission before writing a line of code
- To bring craft to work that could easily be treated as commodity
- To care about outcomes — whether the site actually opens doors — not just delivery

### The aesthetic philosophy

**Restraint with intention.** Not minimalism for its own sake. Not decoration for its own sake. Every element earns its place by serving the communication goal. If removing something makes the page work better, remove it.

This applies to copy, to layout, to motion, to whitespace. The discipline is always: *does this help the visitor understand what they need to understand in order to take the next step?*

### The technical philosophy

**Fast, accessible, honest code.** No bloated frameworks where HTML and CSS will do. No dark patterns. No fake loading animations. No modal popups demanding email addresses. The web is better when it is fast, readable, and works for everyone — including people on slow connections in Nairobi or Bulawayo.

Every site Moyo builds should:
- Load in under 1.5 seconds on a 3G connection
- Pass WCAG AA accessibility standards
- Work without JavaScript for core content
- Be maintainable by the client without technical knowledge

### The relationship philosophy

Moyo takes on a small number of projects at a time. This is not a constraint — it is the product. A client who hires Moyo is not being handed to a junior after the sales call. They have direct access to the person doing the work, throughout the project. That continuity and accountability is what justifies the price.

## 3. Branding & Identity

### Name

**Moyo.** — always with the full stop. The dot is not punctuation. It is a signature. It says: this is the name, this is the practice, this is final.

In running text: *Moyo* (no full stop). In logo/wordmark context: *Moyo.* always.

### Domain & email

- Primary: `moyo.build`
- Email: `hello@moyo.build`
- The `.build` TLD reinforces what the practice does without needing explanation

### Typography

**Display / headlines:** Instrument Serif — a contemporary editorial serif with warmth and authority. Used for all headlines, the wordmark, and pull quotes. Never used for body text.

**Body / UI:** DM Sans — clean, legible, slightly warm. Used for all body copy, navigation, labels, and interface text.

**Avoid:** Inter, Roboto, Arial, system fonts. These signal "default" rather than "considered."

**Scale:**
- Hero headline: `clamp(2.75rem, 7vw, 5.5rem)` — fluid, never static
- Section headline: `clamp(2rem, 4vw, 2.75rem)`
- Body: `1rem / 1.7 line-height`
- Small / labels: `0.75rem`, `letter-spacing: 0.08em`, uppercase, muted

### Colour palette

| Role | Value | Usage |
|------|-------|-------|
| Background | `#faf9f7` | Page background — warm off-white, not stark |
| Surface | `#f1efe9` | Section backgrounds, card fills |
| Surface dark | `#1a1916` | Dark sections — testimonials, footers |
| Text primary | `#1a1916` | All body text |
| Text muted | `#6b6960` | Secondary text, descriptions |
| Text subtle | `#9c9a91` | Labels, metadata, hints |
| Accent | `#c8522a` | CTAs, links, italic emphasis, active states |
| Accent light | `#f5ede8` | Accent backgrounds, featured card fills |
| Border | `rgba(26,25,22,0.1)` | All borders at rest |
| Border strong | `rgba(26,25,22,0.2)` | Hover states, emphasis borders |

**The accent colour (`#c8522a`) is a burnt sienna / terracotta.** It has African earth tones without being clichéd. It is warm without being aggressive. It is distinctive without being loud. It is the single colour that differentiates Moyo from every generic blue or purple web studio.

**What not to do with colour:**
- Do not introduce a second accent colour
- Do not use colour to try to signal "African" through pattern or motif
- Do not use gradients — they signal template
- Do not use colour at all where restraint serves better

### Texture and atmosphere

None. The warmth comes from the off-white background, the serif typography, and the terracotta accent. Adding noise textures or geometric African patterns would undermine the institutional register the work needs to occupy. Moyo's clients are law firms and NGOs — the brand needs to feel at home in their world, which is serious and considered, not expressive.

### Photography

Moyo does not need its own photography. The portfolio pieces are the visual content. When screenshots appear, they should be:
- Hero-cropped to the above-fold section only
- Shown inside a minimal browser chrome (the dot-dot-dot + address bar treatment)
- Never over-compressed — quality signals care

### Logo / wordmark

**Moyo.** — set in Instrument Serif, regular weight, with a terracotta full stop. No icon, no icon mark, no elaborate brandmark. The name is the identity. The dot is the mark.

At very small sizes (favicon, 16px): **M.** in the same typeface, same colour treatment.

### Motion

Use it sparingly and meaningfully:

- Hero elements: staggered entrance on page load (opacity + translateY, 0.7s, ease)
- Cards: subtle translateY(-4px) on hover with a light box-shadow
- Scroll reveals: IntersectionObserver triggering opacity + translateY on work cards
- Nothing else

No scroll-jacking. No parallax. No animated page transitions. These patterns are overused, they break accessibility, and they slow perceived performance.

## 4. Copy & Voice

### The voice

**Direct. Warm. Unimpressed with jargon.**

Moyo writes like a person who knows their work is good and doesn't need to perform confidence. Not arrogant. Not casual. The register of a senior professional who respects the reader's intelligence.

### What this sounds like

| Not this | This |
|----------|------|
| "Leveraging cutting-edge digital solutions" | "Fast websites that work" |
| "We are passionate about helping brands" | "Your work is credible. Your website should be too." |
| "Our bespoke approach ensures" | "The person you speak to is the person who builds your site." |
| "Transformative web experiences" | "Websites that open doors" |
| "I am a results-driven professional" | "I build for African institutions. Here's what that means." |

### Specific copy principles

**Lead with the client's problem, not your capability.** The hero is not about Moyo — it is about the visitor's situation. "Your work is credible. Your website should be too." names the problem before offering a solution.

**Name the audience explicitly.** "African law firms, NGOs, and civil society organisations" is better than "organisations" or "businesses." Specific language makes the right people feel seen and lets the wrong people self-select out.

**Frame location as a strength.** "Working across Africa from Harare" is not a disclaimer. It signals rootedness, understanding, and specificity. Never write "despite being based in Zimbabwe" or "although I work remotely."

**Use outcome language in portfolio descriptions.** Not "I built a website for X." But: "X needed to hold its own in front of London counterparties. Here's what that required."

**Short sentences earn trust.** Long sentences with dependent clauses signal hedging. Write short. Then write the nuance.

**The full stop in the name is the tone.** Moyo. Done. Certain. Move.

### Copy for recurring contexts

**Footer credit on client sites:**
> Built by Moyo. — [moyo.build](https://moyo.build)

**LinkedIn headline:**
> Moyo. — Web development for African law firms, NGOs & professional services · moyo.build

**Email signature:**
> [Your Name]
> Moyo. · moyo.build
> hello@moyo.build

**Cold outreach opening line:**
> "I've been following [Organisation's] work on [specific programme/initiative] — it's serious work that deserves a web presence to match."

Never open with "I hope this email finds you well." Never open with "My name is X and I'm a web developer." Lead with what you know about them.

## 5. Homepage UI & UX

### The single conversion goal

Every element on the page exists to move a visitor toward one action: **sending an email to hello@moyo.build.**

Nothing is on the page unless it serves that goal. No blog feed. No Twitter widget. No awards badge. No counter of cups of coffee consumed.

### Page architecture

```
Header (sticky)
  — Wordmark left / Nav links right / "Start a project" CTA pill
Hero
  — Eyebrow: "Working across Africa from Harare"
  — H1: "Your work is credible. Your website should be too."
  — Body: one sentence on who and what
  — CTAs: primary mailto + ghost "See the work"
  — Proof bar: "Trusted by" + three client names
Work (section 01)
  — Heading: "Three clients. Three sectors. One standard."
  — Spotlight card: WALP Africa (full-width, screenshot left, text right)
  — 2-column grid: Mutema Attorneys + Nyajena Agricultural Group
Services (section 02)
  — Heading: "What it costs, what you get."
  — Three tier cards: Establish / Compete / Grow
  — Footnote on what's included
Testimonials (section 03)
  — Dark background section
  — Three quote cards
Contact (section 04)
  — Heading: "Tell me what you're building."
  — mailto button (large, primary)
  — Response time + time zones note
  — Secondary: LinkedIn + GitHub links
Footer
  — Copyright · Privacy policy · Writing (personal blog link)
```

### Critical UX decisions

**The mailto CTA appears three times:** in the nav (pill), in the hero (primary button), and in the contact section (large button). A visitor who decides on the first scroll still has an immediate action. A visitor who reads everything has it again at the end. Never make someone scroll back to find how to contact you.

**No contact form.** A mailto link converts better for this type of work because it removes friction (no form fields, no CAPTCHA, no "thanks, we'll be in touch"), it puts the conversation in email where professional relationships happen, and it requires no backend or third-party service.

**The proof bar replaces the stat row.** Client names are more credible than category labels like "100% remote-ready." A prospective NGO who recognises WALP Africa is halfway converted before they scroll.

**WALP Africa as spotlight.** The strongest visual piece anchors the work section. It communicates: this is what Moyo produces at its best. Everything else is confirmation.

**Dark testimonials section.** The tonal shift from light to dark signals transition — this is where evidence lives. Dark backgrounds also increase perceived reading comfort for quotes, which are longer text blocks.

**Section labels as navigation anchors.** "01 / Selected work" is not decoration. It tells the visitor where they are in the page and sets expectations for what's coming. It also works as a quiet confidence signal — the organisation of the page reflects the organisation of the thinking.

### Performance requirements

| Metric | Target |
|--------|--------|
| Lighthouse Performance | ≥ 95 |
| First Contentful Paint | < 1.2s |
| Total Blocking Time | 0ms |
| Page weight (ex-images) | < 150KB |
| Image format | WebP, `loading="lazy"` except above-fold |
| Font loading | `display=swap` on Google Fonts |
| JavaScript | < 30 lines, deferred, no libraries |

**The Nyajena SSL certificate must be fixed before the site launches.** A security warning on a linked portfolio piece destroys credibility immediately.

### Accessibility non-negotiables

- Skip link as first body element
- All `<section>` elements have `aria-labelledby` pointing to their `<h2>`
- Only one `<h1>` on the page
- All images have descriptive `alt` text (not "screenshot" or "image")
- All interactive elements reachable by keyboard
- Focus styles never suppressed without replacement
- `<html lang="en">` always set
- Colour contrast ≥ 4.5:1 for body text, ≥ 3:1 for large text

## 6. Marketing Strategy

### The hierarchy of effort

Not all marketing channels return equally. This is the order of priority, from highest to lowest return per hour invested:

1. **Referrals from existing clients** — highest conversion, zero cost
2. **Footer credits on client sites** — passive, compounding, zero ongoing effort
3. **Targeted direct outreach** — high effort, high quality when done right
4. **LinkedIn case studies** — medium effort, long-term network building
5. **Civil society and legal directories** — research-heavy, good lead quality
6. **Everything else** — do not start until the above are working

### Channel 1: Client referrals

The three existing clients — Mutema Attorneys, WALP Africa, Nyajena Agricultural Group — each exist within networks of peer organisations.

**The ask is simple.** Contact each client personally (not via template):

> "I'm taking on new projects this quarter, focused on organisations doing serious work across Zimbabwe and the region. If you know of anyone who you think could use what we built for you, an introduction would genuinely help. Even just a name I could reach out to."

Do this before anything else. Do it this week. A warm introduction from WALP Africa to another feminist NGO arrives pre-qualified and pre-endorsed. Nothing else you can do matches that conversion probability.

**Make it easy for them to refer.** After any successful project, send a short "here's what to say if someone asks about your site" email with one or two sentences they can copy. Reduce the friction to zero.

### Channel 2: Footer credits

Every live client site should have in the footer:

```
Built by Moyo. — moyo.build
```

This is passive lead generation that compounds over time. Every visitor to every client site is a potential lead. The name is intriguing enough to invite a click. The landing page does the conversion work.

Audit all three live sites and update this before the moyo.build site launches.

### Channel 3: Targeted direct outreach

Find organisations with weak websites in your three niches. The signals that indicate a warm lead:

- Listed in a civil society directory or funder database (shows they care about visibility)
- Doing substantive international work (shows they need international-calibre presence)
- Current website is absent, broken, or obviously outdated (shows the gap)
- Organisation is growing or has recently received funding (shows capacity to pay)

**The four-sentence email structure:**

1. *Demonstrate you know their work.* One specific sentence about what they actually do — not "I came across your organisation" but "I've been following your work on [specific programme/initiative]."
2. *Name the gap.* One sentence about what's missing: "Your current website makes it hard for potential partners to understand the scale of what you're doing."
3. *Show proof.* One sentence with a relevant example: "I recently built walpeafrica.org for a Johannesburg-based feminist political organisation — similar mission, similar audience."
4. *Low-commitment ask.* "Would it be useful to have a brief conversation about what a better web presence could do for you?"

No attachment. No pricing. No portfolio PDF. Four sentences. The goal is a reply, not a close.

**Where to find leads:**

- Civicus Civil Society Index — directory of African CSOs
- AWDF (African Women's Development Fund) grantee lists
- Open Society Foundations Africa grantees
- Ford Foundation Africa programme grantees
- Law Society of Zimbabwe member directory — filter for commercial/corporate practices
- Law Society of South Africa — firms doing cross-border Africa work
- Agricultural sector associations: CFU, ZFU, TIMB — member businesses
- Regional professional associations across SADC countries

**Volume and pace:** Ten researched, specific emails per batch. One batch per month. One reply in ten is a good rate. One conversion from that reply is a successful month of outreach.

### Channel 4: LinkedIn

**What works:** Outcome-focused case studies. Not "I build websites." But: "WALP Africa needed to be credible to international donors and inspiring to women leaders simultaneously. Here's how we approached that."

**What doesn't work:** Generic "open for work" posts. Sharing articles about web trends. Commenting "great post!" on things.

**The content cadence:**
- One case study per project (three to start)
- Occasional short observations about web quality in African professional services
- No more than one post per week — quality over frequency

**The connection strategy:** Connect with communications leads, executive directors, and programme directors at organisations similar to your existing clients. Not to pitch — to be visible. When they post about their work, engage specifically. Build recognition over time.

**The organic network effect:** The African civil society and governance space has its own LinkedIn ecosystem. People follow each other, engage with each other's content, and when one needs a website they ask their network. Being a recognised, thoughtful presence in that specific ecosystem is the long game.

### What NOT to do

- **No Upwork, Fiverr, or freelancer platforms.** The clients who will pay $2,000 USD for a website do not hire from these platforms. Your presence there would actively signal the wrong positioning.
- **No mass email blasts.** Generic outreach is deleted before it's read. The four-sentence approach is slower but it actually works.
- **No "just checking in" follow-ups.** One well-crafted email. If no reply after two weeks, move on. The goal is to be remembered well, not to be remembered.
- **No spec work or "let me build you a free landing page to show you what I can do."** This devalues the work and attracts clients who will continue to want free things.

## 7. Converting Traffic from Client Sites

This is the highest-leverage passive channel and most developers under-invest in it.

### The footer credit

It is not enough to put a small grey "website by X" in a footer. The credit should be:

**`Built by Moyo. — moyo.build`**

Specific formatting guidance:
- Use the full stop after Moyo — it's the signature
- Link `moyo.build` — the URL is itself part of the brand statement
- Keep it in the footer nav alongside Privacy Policy — give it the dignity of a real link, not a tiny afterthought
- Colour: muted but legible — not invisible, not competing with the client's brand

### Who clicks footer credits

Not everyone. But the people who do click are disproportionately:
- Communications or marketing staff at other organisations
- Executive directors scouting for vendors
- People who've just seen a site they found impressive and want to know who made it

These are exactly the people Moyo wants. The click-through rate is low but the quality is very high.

### What the landing page must do in five seconds

A visitor from a footer credit arrives with one question: *is this person good enough to build something for me?*

In the first five seconds they need to see:
1. A name that's memorable (Moyo.)
2. A headline that names their problem ("Your work is credible. Your website should be too.")
3. Visual evidence (the WALP Africa screenshot)

If those three things land, they scroll. If they scroll, the rest of the page does the conversion work.

### The specific case for WALP Africa traffic

WALP Africa's audience — civil society workers, programme officers, NGO leadership across Africa — are high-quality leads for Moyo. A visitor from walpeafrica.org arriving at moyo.build sees an NGO site in the portfolio and immediately identifies with it. This is the warmest possible traffic. Make sure the WALP Africa card is prominent and the framing speaks to donors, funders, and civil society partners.

## 8. Client Experience & Process

The quality of the client experience is part of the product. A client who feels heard, informed, and respected throughout the project is a client who refers.

### The intake process

When a new enquiry arrives at hello@moyo.build, respond within one business day. The response should:

1. Acknowledge specifically what they said (prove you read it)
2. Ask the two or three questions needed to understand the project properly
3. Propose a brief call to discuss before any proposal is written

Do not send a pricing menu in the first reply. Do not ask them to fill in a brief form. Have a conversation first.

### The discovery call

Purpose: understand the organisation well enough to write a proposal that addresses their actual problem, not just their stated request.

Questions worth asking:
- Who is the primary audience for the site — funders, clients, partners, the general public?
- What does success look like six months after launch?
- Are there organisations whose websites you admire or whose audiences you want to reach?
- What's your timeline and what's driving it?
- Do you have existing brand guidelines, photography, or copy, or does all of that need to be created?

### The proposal

One page. Not a 20-slide deck. Not a PDF with testimonials and awards. Just:
- What you understood the brief to be (demonstrates listening)
- What you propose to build and why
- What it costs
- What the timeline is
- What you need from them to start

Send it within 48 hours of the discovery call. Proposals that arrive a week later lose momentum.

### During the project

- **One shared document** for feedback (Google Doc works fine) — never manage feedback over email
- **Weekly check-in** — one short message per week on progress, even if there's nothing to report. Silence creates anxiety.
- **Stage gates** — approve content before designing, approve design before building. Never build on unapproved copy.
- **Screenshot updates** — share progress screenshots before formal review stages. Clients who feel included in the making are clients who approve faster.

### Handover

Every project ends with a handover session:
- Screenshare walkthrough of the CMS (if applicable)
- Written guide on how to do the three most common tasks
- Google Search Console verified and connected
- Analytics set up (privacy-respecting: Plausible or Fathom preferred over GA4)
- List of everything the client is responsible for going forward (hosting, domain renewal, content updates)

### Post-project

Three things after every project closes:

1. **Ask for a testimonial** — specifically: "One or two sentences about what the site does for your organisation would mean a lot."
2. **Ask for a referral** — "If you know of any organisation who could use what we built for you, I'd appreciate an introduction."
3. **Check in at three months** — "How's the site performing? Anything you'd like to adjust?" This is not sales — it is good service. It often surfaces a follow-on project organically.

## 9. Pricing Strategy

### Current tiers

| Tier | Price | Positioning |
|------|-------|-------------|
| Establish | From $800 USD | Entry point for smaller orgs needing basic credibility |
| Compete | From $2,000 USD | The real product — institutional-grade presence |
| Grow | By negotiation | Ongoing, retainer, or complex builds |

### Principles

**Price in USD for international clients.** This is standard for professional services billed across currency zones. It protects against ZWL volatility and signals international positioning. Offer ZAR as an alternative for South African clients.

**"From" pricing anchors expectations without locking you in.** A project that starts at "from $2,000" can legitimately land at $3,500 if the scope warrants it — you discussed it upfront, you're not surprising anyone.

**Never discount to close.** Discounting signals that the original price was arbitrary. If a client can't afford the price, offer a reduced scope — fewer pages, no CMS, no SEO setup. Never reduce the price for the same scope. It damages the relationship before the project starts.

**Invoice in three stages for Compete and above:**
- 40% on project commencement
- 40% on design approval
- 20% on launch

This protects you if a project stalls and gives the client clear milestone markers.

**Raise prices after every three projects.** Not dramatically — 10-20% annually. You get better. The market for your work grows. The price should track both.

### When to say no

Some clients will not be the right fit, even if they can pay. Say no to:
- Clients who negotiate aggressively before the project starts (this behaviour intensifies during the project)
- Clients who want "just a quick, simple website" priced at $200 (this expectation does not change)
- Clients whose values conflict with Moyo's — you will spend three months building something you're not proud of
- Clients who can't commit a named internal contact for feedback (projects with no owner on the client side never end)

## 10. Growth Strategy

### Phase 1: Establish (now — 6 months)

Goal: Three live pieces of strong work, one testimonial, moyo.build live with real screenshots, first referral-generated client.

Actions:
- Launch moyo.build
- Update footer credits on all three client sites
- Get one real testimonial from each client
- Send referral asks to all three clients
- Fix Nyajena SSL certificate
- Send five targeted outreach emails to prospects identified in civil society directories

Success metric: First client enquiry that did not come from a personal introduction.

### Phase 2: Build (months 6-18)

Goal: Regular inbound from footer traffic and referrals. Portfolio grows to five strong live pieces. First client outside Zimbabwe or South Africa.

Actions:
- Publish three LinkedIn case studies (one per existing client)
- Systematise the outreach process — one batch of ten researched emails per month
- Begin identifying the next pricing tier increase
- Consider adding one trusted collaborator (designer or copywriter) for larger projects — not a hire, a network relationship

Success metric: Two unsolicited enquiries per month from visitors who found Moyo independently.

### Phase 3: Grow (18 months+)

Goal: Recognised as the go-to studio for institutional web presence in Southern and East Africa. Retainer clients providing stable monthly revenue. Speaking or writing presence in African civil society or legal conferences.

Actions:
- Apply to speak at relevant conferences (African Philanthropy Forum, LSZ events, regional governance summits)
- Develop a lead magnet — a short, practical guide on what makes a credible NGO website — distributed to civil society networks
- Consider a small roster of associate collaborators for larger projects
- Explore whether tech writing for African institutions (annual reports, programme documentation) is a natural adjacent service

Success metric: Retainer revenue covers baseline monthly costs without depending on new project starts.

### The ceiling and the choice

At some point, Moyo will face a choice: stay deliberately small (high quality, high price, low volume, personal) or grow into a multi-person studio.

Both are valid. The boutique path — one person, six to eight projects a year at $2,000-5,000 each — is a comfortable, sustainable, high-quality practice that does not require management or scaling. The studio path requires different infrastructure, different hiring, different systems.

Do not make this choice under pressure. Make it from a position of deliberate abundance. When you have more qualified enquiries than you can serve, you have the luxury of deciding.

## 11. Boutique Practice Best Practices

Lessons from the best independent practices globally, adapted for this context.

### Niche depth compounds

The more deeply Moyo serves African institutions, the harder it becomes for anyone else to compete. Every project generates:
- A new portfolio piece in the niche
- A new potential referrer in the network
- A deeper understanding of what these clients actually need

A generalist starts from zero with every new sector. Moyo starts from accumulated understanding. Protect this by not chasing work outside the niche for short-term revenue.

### One excellent client beats three mediocre ones

The time spent on a difficult $800 client could be spent on finding the right $2,000 client. At the boutique scale, client selection is a business strategy — not just a preference.

### Document everything

Keep a private notes file for every project:
- What the brief actually was (vs. what was written down)
- What decisions were made and why
- What worked, what didn't, what you'd do differently
- Any feedback received during or after the project

This document becomes the brief for the next similar project. It is also the raw material for LinkedIn case studies.

### Protect the work time

The single biggest threat to a boutique practice is fragmentation — spending so much time on business development, admin, and client communication that the actual work suffers.

Practical protection:
- Designate two days per week as "deep work" days — no calls, no email checking before noon
- Batch all client communication into specific time windows
- Use a scheduling link (Calendly, Cal.com) so clients can book calls without email back-and-forth
- Set clear response time expectations (one business day) and meet them consistently

### The reputation is the marketing

At this scale, in this niche, reputation moves faster than any campaign. One outstanding project generates referrals for years. One project that goes badly — a missed deadline, a client who feels ignored, work that falls short — travels just as far.

The best marketing Moyo can do is build fewer, better sites, and treat every client interaction as a reputation-building moment.

### Stay close to the work

As the practice grows, there will be pressure to spend more time on business development and less on building. Resist this as long as possible. The reason clients hire Moyo is that you do the work personally. The moment that stops being true, the fundamental value proposition changes.

## 12. Potential Gotchas & Pitfalls

These are the mistakes most boutique studios make. Know them before making them.

### Scope creep without documentation

A client who liked the site will naturally want "just one more thing." Each individual request seems reasonable. Collectively they can add weeks to a project timeline.

**Prevention:** Write a scope of work document before any project starts. Anything outside that scope is a change request with a price and timeline attached. Be warm about it — "happy to add that, here's what it would cost and add to the timeline" — but be consistent.

### Taking on a client to cover a slow month

The temptation to take on a mismatched project because the pipeline is dry is one of the most common errors in boutique practices. A bad-fit project occupies the time slot that a good-fit project would have filled. It also produces work you don't want in your portfolio.

**Prevention:** Maintain three months of operating costs as a buffer. When that buffer exists, you can afford to say no to the wrong clients.

### Undercharging and then resenting the work

Charging less than the work is worth creates a psychological imbalance that degrades the quality of the relationship. You begin to feel the client owes you something; the client has no idea why the energy shifted.

**Prevention:** Price honestly before starting. If the price feels uncomfortable to say out loud, that is the price. Say it anyway.

### Letting the portfolio go stale

Three pieces of work is enough to launch. It is not enough to sustain for two years. Every six months, Moyo needs either a new portfolio piece or a significantly updated case study of an existing one.

**Prevention:** Build getting new portfolio pieces into the growth strategy. If a project doesn't produce something portfolio-worthy, understand why and adjust the client selection accordingly.

### The "one big client" trap

One client who represents more than 40% of revenue is a business risk — not a success. If they leave, the practice is in crisis. This is especially dangerous with retainer clients, who can feel like stable income until they aren't.

**Prevention:** Diversify actively. Three clients at $2,000 is healthier than one client at $6,000.

### Neglecting the SSL certificate / broken links

Every broken link on the Moyo portfolio page is a silent client you lost. Every SSL error on a linked project undermines credibility before the visitor has read a word. These are trivial to fix and catastrophic to ignore.

**Prevention:** Monthly audit of all linked client sites. Check they load. Check the SSL is valid. Check the footer credit links correctly. This takes fifteen minutes once a month.

### Over-engineering the internal systems

Notion databases, project management tools, CRM systems, proposal software, automated invoicing — these all have their place, but a one-person studio spending three hours a week maintaining project management infrastructure is not building anything.

**Prevention:** Start with the simplest possible system. A Google Doc per project and a shared folder. Upgrade only when the simple version is genuinely failing, not in anticipation of growth.

### Platform dependency

Building every site on WordPress, or any single platform, creates dependency on that platform's continued quality, pricing, and security model. Diversify platform knowledge — maintain fluency in vanilla HTML/CSS/JS (for fast, lightweight builds), one CMS (WordPress or similar), and one hosted CMS option (Webflow, Sanity, or similar) for clients who need to self-edit extensively.

### Promising more than you can deliver

The temptation in a proposal to say "yes" to everything a client asks for is understandable. The cost of over-promising is paid in the project — in stress, in corners cut, in a final product that doesn't match the pitch.

**Prevention:** Write proposals based on what you're confident you can deliver excellently, not what it would take to win the project. The right client will hire you for honesty. The wrong client won't — which is the point.
